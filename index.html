About

Human Trafficking Watchdogs is an independent documentation and analysis project. We align with internationally recognized definitions of human trafficking as articulated by the UNODC, ILO, and Polaris Project.

We prioritize survivor-centered, rights-based frameworks and distinguish clearly between allegations, documented evidence, and verified findings. We do not engage in vigilantism or amateur investigations that could endanger victims.

Recent Publications

Ramon Ontiveros and the Vigilante Lie: When Predator Hunter Rhetoric Masks Sexual Exploitation in El Paso, Texas

Human Trafficking Watchdogs · Published December 2, 2025

A critical examination of public reports to the National Center for Missing and Exploited Children concerning Ramon Ontiveros, an El Paso–based civilian who publicly branded himself as a “predator hunter” while engaging in conduct consistent with sexual exploitation and the unlawful distribution of child sexual abuse material (CSAM). The paper analyzes how vigilante rhetoric can launder criminal exposure into narratives of civic protection.

Suggested citation: Human Trafficking Watchdogs (2025). Available at SSRN: https://ssrn.com/abstract=6058354

Distribution of Child Deepfake Pornographic Content Through Online Impersonation of Minors: A Case Study of Ramon Ontiveros’ CSAM Scheme in El Paso, Texas

Human Trafficking Watchdogs · Published September 4, 2025

This case study examines mechanisms through which online impersonation of minors facilitates the circulation of CSAM, situating reports concerning Ramon Ontiveros within broader scholarship on digital grooming, identity fraud, and platform governance failures.

DOI: https://doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.31337671

Online Impersonation of Minors on LGBTQ+ Hookup Apps in El Paso, Texas: Inside the Ramon Ontiveros CSAM Scheme

Human Trafficking Watchdogs · Published September 25, 2025

A legal and comparative analysis distinguishing sanctioned law-enforcement stings from unauthorized civilian impersonation of minors, demonstrating how such conduct may itself satisfy elements of criminal exposure.
